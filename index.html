If you don't put art on your website people will die.

By Annie Swanson · February 25, 2026

The Philosophy

Art should be implemented into websites and digital interfaces for the well being of humans. A practice of implementing art into digital interfaces needs to be developed in the culture of doing business so that the interfaces of the future will contain elements of humanity and what is good about humans won’t wither.

I appreciate art as much as the next artist, but my reason for wanting it to be implemented into digital life are not aesthetic, they are commercial and altruistic. I’m concerned about art and randomness not being involved in current digital interfaces, and even more concerned about it not being involved in BCI technology. I think art is the most characteristic way to represent the ethos of a company in separation from others. Most people I’ve talked to about this have told me that paintings don’t go on websites, or that if I want to work with art I should go do it in the fine art world, or that the economics of having art made for websites don’t work out, or that artists don’t want their art on websites. Basically, I’ve heard a lot of “that’s not how it works…” But, I want to break how it works.

Defining Art

I have a very broad definition of art. But what I’m not necessarily talking about is visual garnish for the sake of decoration. My definition of art centers around anything made with a high level of accumulated care and for the sake of itself. I always liked Oscar Wilde’s take on art being purely for its own sake. It’s the only thing in the world that doesn't serve a purpose. And that is why it illuminates our souls when we encounter it, because we go about our lives interacting with lots of other purposeful things. So art is a refuge, a rest, a treat, a sanctuary.

I’m not so picky about the medium either. My personal taste leans toward paintings. But there are incredible things being done in the digital mediums of 3D art, motion design, photography, illustrations, coding, etc. I recently worked with a design engineer, Lorenzo Dossi, on a project to hard code gradient hue shifts into a website. I’d consider that art, I think it's something exciting and new that is being made in the world. Lorenzo developed a tool so others can make art in this way. The art sits on the homepage of an AI healthcare startup’s website. Since the site has art implemented into it, I think that visiting that website is more pleasant and healthy for the visitor as well as advantageous to the company’s brand. It’s a small detail. It’s a lot of work to get done. But I think, long term, it’s worth it.

The Stakes

The way that our brains currently work involves a lot of unconscious input. On your commute to work you see a baby in a stroller, a dog peeing on a fire hydrant, a crossing guard directing traffic, the people in the subway, the door of your office building. Not to mention all that you hear and smell. You probably don’t consider this input very consciously. But it is important to the way that your brain functions.

When we began building digital tools for efficiency and convenience, this unconscious input did not seem important. It’s not important to a map app on your phone if getting you where you want to go is the goal, nor a banking website on your computer to perform your banking tasks. After decades of our digital interfaces making us feel less than human, I wonder why we don’t often consider the strictness of necessary information and realize that it is not natural to the way that human brains operate.

As time goes on, I predict that we will become increasingly merged with the digital interfaces that assist our lives. Eventually, we will interact with the world through brain computer interfaces. I think a few important questions to ask are: If BCI is optimized for efficiency alone, what does a mind feel like when it has no exposure to irregularity? Does randomness preserve cognitive elasticity? What would happen to boredom and imagination? If we think about pre-linguistic self-censorship, might art be a nice pre-linguistic resting place? Can art be created cognitively and “exported”? There are lots of other questions that will need to be addressed when we enter into the building of BCI on a mass scale. If “mistakes” in thinking are corrected what happens to creativity? We don’t want our thoughts to be smoothed and perfected. We don’t want to lose boredom, chaos, tangents, awe, or mistakes. If you were to create a system for inefficiency and irregularity that might be essential to positive evolution of human thought, then I argue art as infrastructure would do the job.

The Future

There is no doubt that our experiences as humans are becoming more and more digital. I don’t think this will reverse. Imagine in thirty years when we are even more merged with our computers than we are now. Right now when we have a question we want to search we must pull out the phone, unlock it, navigate to an app, type the question, and read the answer. The building of computer technology has trended toward less and less friction in each new iteration of it. So I imagine that in thirty years our processes of accessing and using our technology will have even less friction. We might be able to wonder a question and have the answer before the question is even fully formed. It’s not so obvious, but friction is essential to thought. Imagine your mind without the act of wondering. I think a lot of people might look forward to the time when humans can plug the instantaneous and vast power of computers into their minds. And I agree that this will be interesting. I think it will create a really efficient human. But I don’t think it will create a creative, expansive, satisfied human. We need to be delayed sometimes, to encounter obstacles and overcome them, and to wander.

I also think about what the internet will become in its next iteration after AI agents are swimming throughout it. And then the iteration after that when text or language is no longer needed for communication. Computers don’t communicate with each other through language. Humans must break thought down into language to communicate with one another. But in a future where thought can theoretically be communicated directly from brain to brain, I wonder what will become of an internet that is currently primarily text/ language based. I imagine that the internet will look entirely different than it does now. I wonder what role art might play in a post-text internet. So, looking forward to a future when art is very important to the development of our fabric of the internet and the ways that we communicate, I think it makes sense to begin implementing art into the forms of technology that we currently have. So that when technologies of the future arrive, we have a basis for how this should be done. It should become culturally unacceptable for major technology firms to operate without funding and embedding living artists into their digital architecture. Every tech company should have a handful of artists in residence.

A Call for Similar Thinking

If you don’t see the value in this, please let me know why. It seems obvious to me, but it is not to everyone. I’m also on a journey of searching for anyone else out there who is thinking about the same things as I am, so if you know of anyone I’d love to be put in touch.

The Application

With all of this in mind, I propose implementing art into the websites and interfaces of today.

Where I'm Coming From

Where I’m coming from: I’ve worked between the fine art world in galleries and early-stage social media and AI tech startups. Now I run the business side of a small design studio in lower manhattan. I recently curated a database of over 2,000 creatives and creative agencies that I have worked with or know of. I have relationships with artists and I understand how they can be of use in the world of technology. I am bilingual in artistic intuition and commercial execution.

What this Could Look Like for Any Firm

Here are some very quick examples of what art on a simple website might look like. For more complicated websites, websites that must express more information, digital products, interfaces, and apps I aslo have ideas of how the art can be implemented even if it is not the centerpiece. Also want to note that these mockups are a blatant misuse of art that belongs to these artists. The works included are used for purposes of illustration and explanation only.

My Proposal

I art direct the process of sourcing the right piece(s) of art for your firm and the work with a designer to implement the art into the site (and other places if you wish) properly.

Methods

  1. You can commission a career artist to create a work. This will be expensive. Artists who are doing well don’t often take on commissions—they’d prefer to choose their subject matter.
  2. Buy an existing work from a career artist. This will be expensive. The difficult part is the search. You must hold the search to as high of a standard as it requires.
  3. Commission an amateur artist. The quality of work can be unpredictable, but you might have some power over subject matter. Agreeing upon rounds of revisions, level of art direction, and subject upfront is helpful.
  4. Buy an existing work from an amateur artist. This is also about the search. It’s difficult, but gems are out there. It is inexpensive and easy to acquire.
  5. Find a work in the public domain. This is becoming increasingly fun with tools like Cosmos Public Work, and it can work well. However, there is no guarantee that your chosen work won’t appear in lots of other places that might not be advantageous to your website or company's brand.
  6. Generate something. This is easy, fast, and cheap. But there is an obvious loss in quality as well as emotional attachment to the work that you will suffer—and your viewers will feel—even though they don’t know exactly why.
  7. Create a work yourself. This is ideal if you are a great artist. Disadvantageous if not.

Frameworks

There are a few different ways of thinking about the use of art that I might come to in the process of choosing or directing the art. They include, but are not limited to:

  • A single, canonical artwork permanently associated with a firm.
  • A changing archive that evolves over time.
  • Commissioned portraiture that situates humans inside narrative.
  • Art inserted in places where it interrupts utility on purpose.
  • Reclaiming works with new meaning.

What this Can Do for Any Firm

Implementing art can give any firm a singular, recognizable identity that is not like any other firm’s identity. Representing yourself with great art made by humans shows tangibly, if not obviously, that you are interested in human creation and humans who are creating things in the world. The firms that embed living art into their digital presence will outlast those that optimize purely for clarity.
